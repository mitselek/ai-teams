# Named Concepts Beat Descriptive Phrases for Cross-Artifact References

When the same idea is referenced across multiple artifacts (prompts, wiki entries, scratchpads), a named concept (e.g., "Runtime team dir", "path anchoring") is superior to a descriptive phrase (e.g., "the ephemeral one platform-managed", "the discipline of anchoring paths").

**Three advantages of named concepts:**
1. **Citable across artifacts** without re-explaining.
2. **Reusable without ambiguity** — the name is the definition's handle.
3. **Harder to misremember** than multi-word prose descriptions.

**Naming signal:** You find yourself describing something with a multi-word phrase more than once across different contexts. That's the trigger to name it.

**Canonical example:** Cal's 5 named concepts in `wiki/gotchas/dual-team-dir-ambiguity.md` ("Repo team config dir", "Runtime team dir", "bare path", "anchored path", "path anchoring") replaced Celes's original descriptive phrases. Three artifacts aligned (wiki gotcha + Eratosthenes prompt + Callimachus prompt) using the named terms. The alignment was friction-free precisely because the names were citable; descriptive phrases required re-reading context to parse.
